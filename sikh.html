Sikhism

Overview Sects The Five K's Sikh Holidays

Sikhism is the ninth largest religion in the world with 30 million practitioners worldwide that mostly live in the Indian state of Punjab.

It is a monotheistic religion founded by Guru Nanak Dev in Punjab in the 15th century. Sikhs call their faith Gurmat (Punjabi: "the Way of the Guru"). According to Sikh tradition, it was founded by Guru Nanak and then led by a succession of nine other Gurus- it is believed that all ten Gurus were inhabited by a single spirit. Upon the death of the tenth Guru (Guru Gobind Singh) in 1708, the spirit transferred itself to the sacred scripture that Sikhs follow called Guru Granth Sahib ("The Granth as the Guru"), also known as the Adi Granth ("First Volume"). This is considered to be the only Guru now.

Sects:

There are a couple sects of Sikhism, but here are three significant ones.
The Namdhari sect believes that Guru Granth Sahib was not appointed to be the the tenth Guru- rather, Balak Singh of Hazro was nominated instead. The Namdhari succession includes Ram Singh, Hari Singh, Partap Singh, and Jagjit Singh. Namdharis commonly believe that Ram Singh (who was exiled from India by the British in 1872) is still alive and will return to be a leader once again. They revere the Guru Granth and Dasam Granth, and are strict vegetarians that only drink rainwater (or water from a well, river, or lake). They have a distinct style of dress that includes white oval turbans and kachhera, and they never wear black or blue.

The Nirankari movement is based on the teachings of Baba Dyal, who was against idolatry and emphasized Nirankar (the formless aspect of the divine). It started with Gautam Singh and has had several successors including Darbar Singh, Sahib Rattaji, and Gurdit Singh. They prohibit the use of alcohol and tobacco, and do not bury or cremate their dead- rather, they place the corpse in flowing river water. Tensions in 1978 resulted in the martyrdom of 13 Sikhs, including their leader, Bhai Fauja Singh.


Radha Soami , also known as Radha Satsang or Radha Soami, is a spiritual movement with about 2 million members founded by Shiv Dayal Singh Seth in 1869. They do not consider themselves to be Sikhs, but they use Guru Granth Sahib as their holy scripture. They are not initiated into Sikhism through the Amrit ceremony, but they do adhere to a vegetarian lifestyle and abstain from alchohol and other intoxicants. The ultimate goal of life according to this movement is to merge with the ultimate divine reality, or Soami.


The Five Ks:

Kesh - long hair covered by a turban
Kangha - a small wooden comb to keep the hair combed twice a day
Kara- iron bangle worn on the hand used most
Kachera - a specific undergarment for men & women
Kirpan - a short dagger






Holidays and Festivals

Vaisakhi
Every April 13th,the anniversary of the founding of the Khalsa Order which is Guru Gobind's devout warrior saints is celebrated. On this day, Sikhs visit temples and hold fairs and parades.
Bandi Chhor Divas
This holiday celebrates Guru Hargobind's release from imprisonment and welcome home in Amritsar in 1619. The holiday involves illuminating temples where temples have to be well lit. Depending on the lunar calendar, it is celebrated the end of October or beginning of November. Candies and sweets are distributed out in gurdwaras.
Maghi
On January 14th, the Sikhs pay tribute to the 40 immortals that gave their life in the battle against the Mughal army. Their deaths are significant because they had abandoned Guru Gobind Singh but redeemed themselves with bravery later on. Guru Gobind Singh declared that they had achieved liberation from death and will live on as immortals and blessed them.
