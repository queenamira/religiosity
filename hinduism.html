Hinduism

Overview Gods Goddesses Holidays and Festivals

Hinduism is a widely professed faith in India but is also found in Bangladesh, Nepal, and Guyana. According to some scholars, Hinduism is the oldest living religion in the world. It has many sacred texts written in Sanskrit and other similar languages. Hinduism has had a dominant presence in Southeast Asia for more than a thousand years starting in the 4th century CE. In the early 21st century, it had nearly one billion adherents worldwide and was the religion of about 80 percent of India’s population.

Hindus are followers of Hinduism. They believe in various gods and goddesses.

Hindu gods

The Hindu triumvirate (Tridevs) consists of three gods who are responsible for the creation, upkeep and destruction of the world. They are Shiva, Vishnu, and Brahma. There is also Ganesha, who is not part of the Tridevs.




Lord Vishnu is the preserver and protector of the universe. His role is to return to the earth in troubled times and restore the balance of good and evil. He has been reincarnated nine times. His worshippers, the Vaishnava, consider him the greatest god and only worship him. Vishnu monotheism is called Vaishnavism.





Brahma's job was the creation of the world and all the creatures in it. He is the least worshipped today out of all the gods and India has only 2 temples devoted to him.





Lord Shiva's role is to destroy the universe in order to recreate it. His wife Parvati keeps him in balance- they are praised as a power couple.




Ganesh is the god of wisdom, success, and good luck. Hindu tradition states that by worshiping Ganesha, one can remove all obstacles and difficulties.




Lord Krishna is worshipped as the eighth incarnation of the Hindu god Vishnu and also as a supreme god in his own right. With his flute, he plays soulful music.



Hindu Goddesses

The Hindu Tridevis are the feminine counterparts of the Tridevs (the Triumvirate). They include Lakshmi, Parvati, and Saraswati. Kali is not a part of the Tridevis.


Mata Durga is known as the demon king slayer. She is also the representation of feminine power in the universe. She is shown as having multiple hands and holding a trishul (trident).



Mata Parvati was born a mortal and is the reincarnation of Shiva’s past wife. Her devotion to and worship of Shiva led her to become his wife and attain the power of shakti (cosmic energy that dynamic forces have). She’s the goddess of fertility, love and devotion- she represents gentleness and nurturing.



Mata Lakshmi is the goddess of wealth, abundance, and fertility. She is the wife of Vishnu and reincarnates alongside her consort on Earth.







Mata Saraswati is the goddess of wisdom, music, and learning. She is the wife of Brahma. Saraswati is also revered by by believers of the Jain religion, as well as some Buddhist sects. She is often depicted as a beautiful woman dressed in pure white, usually seated on a white lotus- she embodies the experience of the highest reality.






Mata Kali is the goddess of death and doomsday. She is also a version of the goddess Shakti just like Parvati and she is the wife of Shiva. She is known as the demon slayer because she engaged in a battle with a demon and drank his blood. She became aggressive and destructive after that. As a way of protecting the world from her anger, Shiva laid under her feet to calm her. She became embarrassed and realized her mistake. As a result, she bites her tongue.


Hindu Holidays and Festivals

Holi, which is also known as the festival of colors, signals the arrival of spring after winter and signifies the victory of good over evil. It is celebrated as a day of spreading happiness and love. Friends and families go around and spread colors on each other's faces.


Raksha Bandhan means "The Bond of Protection". It celebrates brotherhood and love. The occasion means the knot of protection. The Rakhi festival happens this day and it symbolizes the eternal love of brother-sister relationship which isn't just blood related. Sikhs also celebrate it because it is part of Indian culture. A sister ties a rakhi (holy thread) around the wrist of her brother in order to pray for his prosperity, health and well-being. The brother in return offers gifts and promises to protect his sister from any harm and under every circumstance. The festival is also celebrated between brothers and sisters belonging to distant family members, friends, relatives or cousins.




Diwali, or the Festival of Lights is a major holiday that is celebrated in India. It lasts 5 days and celebrates the victory of good over evil and the triumph of light over darkness. In India, it is also enjoyed by non-Hindu communities, including Muslims, Buddhists, Jains, and Sikhs. During Diwali, families and friends share sweets and gifts and there is also a custom of giving food and goods to those in need. It is also traditional for homes to be cleaned and new clothes to be worn at the time of the festival. Families light diyas (oil lamps), make rangoli (art created on the floor with colored sand), and watch fireworks.



Dussehra is a festival in which Hindus celebrate the victory of good over evil. They celebrate by putting tika on their forehead, praying, and practicing religious rituals like burning an effigy of Ravana (devil king of Lanka).



Navratri is a Hindu autumn festival that lasts for 9 days. The goddess Durga is worshipped throughout these 9 days. It also signifies good over evil because Durga killed the bull demon. Hindus stay awake the whole night and play dandiya (traditional folk dance involving stick objects) and garba (folk dance).




Ganesha Chaturthi is the celebration of the birth of Ganesha, son of Shiva and Parvati. The festival may last 10 days or may be celebrated at home. It is celebrated by chanting Vedic hymns and Hindu texts and prayers.





Krishna Janmashtami: A two day festival celebrating the birth of Lord Krishna. It is observed by conducting dances and plays, night vigil, fasting, and having puja (prayer ritual).
